Professional Background

I studied phylotranscriptomics in Dr. Seth Bybee's lab from April 2014 to the present. In April 2015, I will graduate from Brigham Young University (BYU) with a bachelor's degree in bioinformatics and minors in mathematics and computer science.

In my early days of college, I did some simple mathematics research with Ore's Closure on Hamiltonian graphs. At the 2013 BYU CPMS Student Research Conference, I gave a short presentation on my research.

I have interests in evolutionary biology and phylogenetics. In the Bybee Lab, we have researched the evolution of opsin genes in dragonflies. My contribution has been the "babysitting" of transcriptome data through trimming, assembly, annotation, ortholog clustering, and tree construction. In this process, I have utilized multithreading in python to take advantage of the campus supercomputer. It is amazing how much faster a computer can gather data from 30,000 files in the same directory when it uses 32 threads! Although I have only a little training in algorithm analysis, I am becoming skilled at choosing data structures that optimize for high speed or low memory. We are close to publishing a paper demonstrating a novel method for detecting false-positive homology. In the future, I hope to develop methods for inferring the gender of a specimen given a transcriptome.

For fun, I wrote a small program to model the effects of non-random mating on allele frequencies in a simplistic population.
